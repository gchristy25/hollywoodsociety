Film Aggregators vs Distributors: What's the Difference and Which Do You Need?

HollywoodSociety Staff • Film Distribution

When independent filmmakers start researching how to get their films onto streaming platforms, two terms come up repeatedly: aggregators and distributors. These are not the same thing, and understanding the difference between film aggregators vs distributors can save you time, money, and frustration. Here's a clear breakdown of what each one does and which approach is likely to serve your film best.

What Is a Film Aggregator?

A film aggregator is a service that handles the technical delivery of your film to digital platforms. Aggregators have encoding facilities and platform relationships that allow them to format and deliver your content to services like iTunes, Amazon, Google Play, and others.

The key thing to understand about aggregators is that they are primarily a delivery service. They get your film onto platforms, but their involvement typically ends there. Most aggregators do not negotiate deals on your behalf, market your film, or develop a distribution strategy. They encode your assets, deliver them to the platforms you select, and charge a fee for the service.

Aggregator fee structures vary. Some charge a flat upfront fee per platform. Others take a percentage of revenue. Some combine both models. In all cases, you're paying for access and delivery, not for strategic distribution.

What Is a Film Distributor?

A film distributor is a company that acquires the distribution rights to your film and takes responsibility for placing it across platforms, negotiating deals, managing marketing, and maximizing revenue. A distributor functions as your film's business partner in the marketplace.

Distributors maintain relationships with a wide range of platforms and buyers, both domestic and international. They negotiate licensing deals, manage release strategies across different windows and revenue models, handle technical delivery, create or oversee marketing materials, and report revenue back to filmmakers.

The distributor model is built on the idea that an experienced company with strong platform relationships and market knowledge can generate more revenue for your film than you could on your own. In exchange, the distributor earns a commission on the revenue your film generates.

Key Differences Between Aggregators and Distributors

Strategy and Negotiation

This is the most fundamental difference. An aggregator delivers your film to platforms. A distributor develops and executes a strategy to maximize your film's performance across those platforms. Distributors negotiate deal terms, choose the right release timing, and make strategic decisions about which platforms and territories to prioritize.

When a distributor like Octane Multimedia places a film on Netflix, Hulu, or DirecTV, that placement comes through a negotiated relationship, not simply a delivery pipeline. The terms of those deals directly impact how much revenue your film generates.

Marketing Support

Aggregators typically do not market your film. Once it's delivered to a platform, it's up to you to drive viewership. Distributors, on the other hand, invest in marketing efforts that can include trailer creation, key art development, social media campaigns, press outreach, and platform-specific promotional strategies.

Marketing can make an enormous difference in a film's performance. A title that's actively promoted will almost always outperform an identical title that's simply uploaded to a platform without any support.

Platform Access

Aggregators give you access to open platforms that accept content through delivery pipelines. Distributors can get your film onto platforms that aggregators simply cannot access. Many premium platforms and buyers work exclusively with established distributors, meaning some of the most valuable placements are only available through a distribution partner.

Revenue Optimization

Aggregators place your film where you tell them to. Distributors analyze the market and determine where your film will perform best. This includes decisions about windowing (the order in which your film appears on different platform types), territorial strategy, and pricing. These decisions directly impact total revenue.

When Does an Aggregator Make Sense?

Aggregators can be appropriate for filmmakers who have very specific and limited distribution goals, such as getting a single title onto one or two platforms where they plan to handle their own marketing. They can also work for filmmakers who have already secured their own deals and just need a delivery mechanism.

However, for filmmakers who want their film to reach the widest possible audience and generate maximum revenue, the aggregator-only approach has significant limitations.

When Is a Distributor the Better Choice?

For most independent filmmakers, a distributor provides substantially more value than an aggregator. If you want your film on major platforms, in international markets, and across multiple revenue streams, a distributor is equipped to make that happen.

A distributor is particularly valuable when your film has genuine commercial potential. Films with strong genre appeal, professional production quality, and marketable elements benefit most from the strategic approach a distributor brings.

Making the Right Choice for Your Film

The choice between a film aggregator and a distributor comes down to what you need. If you just need files delivered to a specific platform, an aggregator may suffice. If you want a strategic partner who will actively work to maximize your film's reach and revenue, a distributor is the way to go.
